Overview

The animations on this page illustrate our continuation framework for optimal low-thrust trajectory design, which couples pseudo-arclength continuation (PALC) with a stationary-condition–based termination (SCBT) criterion. In each stage, we track a zero-curve of a parameterized shooting function and halt exactly when the transversality (stationary) condition for the active continuation parameter is satisfied—yielding an optimal solution to the final desired problem.

Results are showcased for two of the three examples from the paper. First, a minimum-fuel transfer between L2\text{L}_2L2​ and L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo orbits, where the videos step through freeing the terminal-halo parameter (s1)f(s_1)_f(s1​)f​, the initial-halo parameter (s1)0(s_1)_0(s1​)0​, and finally the final time tft_ftf​; the resulting solution reorganizes thrust/coast structure and exploits the unstable invariant manifold of the initial L2 halo orbit. Second, a minimum-time transfer from GTO to an L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo's stable manifold, where an initial stage transitions from a local maximum solution of the initial problem to a local minimum, before successive stages free s2s_2s2​, the initial true-longitude parameter sωs_\omegasω​ (ω0,true=2πsω\omega_{0,\text{true}}=2\pi s_\omegaω0,true​=2πsω​), and the initial true anomaly parameter sθs_\thetasθ​ (θ0=2πsθ\theta_0=2\pi s_\thetaθ0​=2πsθ​).

Collectively, the clips make visible how the trajectories and optimal control cost functions change while each parameter is freed from a solution to an initial “fixed-parameter” problem to a solution of the desired ones. In each animation, the panels showing the SCBT function (i.e., cic_ici​ i∈{0,1,2,3}i\in\{0,1,2,3\}i∈{0,1,2,3}) and the corresponding optimal-control cost (i.e., Δm\Delta mΔm or Δt\Delta tΔt) include a moving marker that traces the current solution point along the zero curve, synchronized with all other panels.

Transfer Between Halo Orbits with Minimum-Fuel

This example corresponds to the minimum-fuel L2\text{L}_2L2​ to L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo orbit transfer discussed in Section IV-B of the paper. The sequence of animations illustrates how the stationary-condition–based continuation method transitions the initial fixed-parameter problem to the desired problem formulation. Each stage frees one parameter of the optimal control problem while pseudo-arclength continuation tracks the corresponding zero-curve and terminates automatically at points satisfying the transversality condition corresponding to the current continuation parameter.

Continuation of (s1)f(s_1)_f(s1​)f​ parameter

This first stage frees the terminal-halo parameter (s1)f(s_1)_f(s1​)f​, allowing the trajectory to converge toward the optimal arrival location on the L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo. As shown in the animation, the insertion point on the target halo orbit gradually shifts as the continuation parameter is varied, leading to noticeable changes in the overall trajectory. The placement and timing of the thrust arcs also evolve throughout the process, as evident in the top-center panel.

Animation showing the continuation of the (s1)f parameter for transfer between L2 and L1 halo orbits

Continuation of s0s_0s0​ parameter

The second stage frees the initial-halo parameter (s1)0(s_1)_0(s1​)0​, enabling optimization of both the departure from the L2\text{L}_2L2​ orbit and the arrival at the L1\text{L}_1L1​ orbit. As the continuation parameter (s1)0(s_1)_0(s1​)0​ is varied, the exit point from the initial halo orbit moves steadily, producing corresponding adjustments in the overall trajectory. Interestingly, the final insertion point into the target halo also shifts to remain optimal—ultimately returning toward the initially prescribed location.

Animation showing the continuation of the (s1)0 parameter for transfer between L2 and L1 halo orbits

Continuation of tft_ftf​ parameter

In the final stage, the continuation frees the final-time parameter tft_ftf​, completing the transition to the fully optimal transfer solution. As tft_ftf​ increases along the zero curve, the departure point from the L2\text{L}_2L2​ halo orbit changes significantly, and the structure of the thrust arcs adapt accordingly. One thrust arc disappears entirely at the point where the slope of the c2c_2c2​ is discontinuous, while the initial thrust arc diminishes until it closely resembles an impulsive maneuver.

Transfer from GTO to an L1 Halo Orbit in Minimum-Time

This example corresponds directly to the minimum-time GTO L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo stable manifold transfer presented in Section IV-C of the paper. The animations display the evolution of the trajectory throughout the continuation process in which the parameters s1s_1s1​, s2s_2s2​, sωs_\omegasω​ (ω0,true=2πsω\omega_{0,\text{true}}=2\pi s_\omegaω0,true​=2πsω​), and sθs_\thetasθ​ (θ0=2πsθ\theta_0=2\pi s_\thetaθ0​=2πsθ​) are adjusted. Each stage traces the corresponding zero-curve using pseudo-arclength continuation and terminates automatically when the transversality condition is satisfied. The progression clearly shows how the spiral geometry evolves and the transfer time decreases as the continuation advances toward the optimal minimum-time solution of the desired problem.

Continuation of s1s_1s1​ parameter

The first stage adjusts s1s_1s1​, transitioning the trajectory to the optimal insertion point into the prescribed cross-section of the L1\text{L}_1L1​ halo’s stable manifold. As noted in the paper, this stage moves from a local maximum of the initial problem to a nearby local minimum. In the animation, the Earth-centered spirals visibly rotate as the final insertion point moves along the cross-section, and the terminal coast on the stable manifold adjusts accordingly.

Animation showing the continuation of the s1 parameter for transfer from GTO to an L1 halo orbit

Continuation of s2s_2s2​ parameter

Freeing s2s_2s2​ then refines the insertion onto the stable manifold. Only modest geometric changes are observed, but the Earth-centered revolutions show slight rotation, and the insertion point shifts closer to Earth, consistent with the increase in s2s_2s2​ and incremental improvement in terms of the cost function as reported in the paper and shown in the right panel.

Animation showing the continuation of the s2 parameter for transfer from GTO to an L1 halo orbit

Continuation of sωs_\omegasω​ (ω0,true=2πsω\omega_{0,\text{true}}=2\pi s_\omegaω0,true​=2πsω​) parameter

This stage frees the initial true-longitude of perigee parameter sωs_\omegasω​, allowing the GTO’s orientation to vary. As sωs_\omegasω​ changes, the Earth-centered spirals rotate markedly–briefly reversing direction as PALC traverses folds on the zero-curve–and the final coast along the stable manifold decreases.

Animation showing the continuation of the sω parameter for transfer from GTO to an L1 halo orbit

Continuation of sθs_\thetasθ​ (θ0=2πsθ\theta_0 = 2\pi s_\thetaθ0​=2πsθ​) parameter

The final stage frees the true-anomaly parameter sθs_\thetasθ​, completing the continuation to the desired minimum-time solution. Only minor rotation in the spirals are visible, consistent with the small additional reduction in time of flight achieved in this step.
